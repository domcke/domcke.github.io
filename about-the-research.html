About our Research

Transcription factors (TFs) determine cell fates by regulating which genes are turned on or off in a given cell. A central aim of our research is to be able to predict TF activity in any given cell, even if it is not naturally expressed there. This would enable us to perform intentional cell state transitions, for example from diseased to healthy states.

Learning mechanisms of TF binding site selection

TFs recognize defined sequence motifs in the DNA, yet they only occupy a small subset of these sites in a cell type-specific manner. We are systematically studying the rules underlying cell type-specific TF occupancy and its downstream effects.

Building and leveraging predictive models of TF activity

We are not just focusing on naturally occurring cell state transitions, but exploring and mapping the landscape of possible stable cell states attainable by natural TFs. We are using these maps to train deep learning models and predict TF 'recipes' for intentional novel cell state transitions.

Transferability across scales and organisms

Being able to predict cell state transitions in patients would have enormous therapeutic potential. Our experiments are performed in cells in a dish, which differ drastically from a complex organism. To bridge this gap, we are studying the influence of cell-cell interactions in multicellular systems and transferring TF activity predictions across environments and species.

Our tools

Experimental: in vitro models of various cell types, genetic and epigenetic editing technologies, high-throughput pooled screens and single cell genomics assays.

Computational: causal, mechanistic models and predictive deep learning models.
